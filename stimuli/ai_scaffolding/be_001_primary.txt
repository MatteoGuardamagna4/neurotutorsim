TUTOR: Let's work through a break-even problem together. A company has
fixed costs of EUR 120,000 per year. Each unit sells for EUR 80 and costs
EUR 50 in variable costs. How many units must it sell to break even? There
is a formula for this, but I want to see how you'd approach it first. Give
it a try and show me your work before I say anything else.

LEARNER: That's easy, 120,000 divided by 80 is 1,500. So the company needs
to sell 1,500 units to break even.

TUTOR: Hint: before you divide, remember that part of each unit's EUR 80
price pays for the variable cost of actually making that unit. Only the
leftover portion helps cover fixed costs, so dividing by the full price
skips a step. Think about what happens to each euro of revenue the moment
a unit is sold, some of it is already spoken for.

LEARNER: Oh, so I shouldn't divide fixed costs by the full price?

TUTOR: Right, not by the full price. What do we call the leftover amount
after variable cost is subtracted from price, and how would you calculate
it for this problem?

LEARNER: Is that the contribution margin? EUR 80 minus EUR 50?

TUTOR: Hint: that subtraction is exactly the right move. EUR 80 minus EUR
50 gives you the contribution margin per unit. Use that number, not the
raw price, as the divisor in the break-even formula. This margin is the
part of every sale that is actually free to go toward paying down fixed
costs, everything else was already spent on production.

LEARNER: Okay, so EUR 80 minus EUR 50 is EUR 30. Then EUR 120,000 divided
by EUR 30?

TUTOR: That's the right setup. Go ahead and finish it, what does EUR
120,000 divided by EUR 30 come out to?

LEARNER: EUR 120,000 divided by EUR 30 is 4,000. So 4,000 units.

TUTOR: That's correct, 4,000 units is the break-even quantity. Notice why
your first attempt of 1,500 came out too low: it treated the whole EUR 80
price as if it all went toward fixed costs, when EUR 50 of it actually
covers production cost. Only the remaining EUR 30 contribution margin per
unit helps pay off the EUR 120,000 in fixed costs, which is why the margin,
not the price, belongs in the denominator. Carry that same margin-first
habit into the next practice problem: subtract variable cost from price
before you divide anything into fixed costs.
